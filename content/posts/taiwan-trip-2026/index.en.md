I'd been meaning to go to Taiwan for years. Kept putting it off. Probably because it felt too close — culturally close. Same written language, same folk religions, same lunar calendar rhythms. It never felt urgent.

This March we finally went. Ten days. Taipei to Chiayi to Tainan and back. My wife handled the restaurant bookmarks and attraction list; I handled the history research and logistics. We've been doing this long enough that the division of labor works without discussion.

## 🌧️ Taipei

Taoyuan airport is small. We were out fast. The driver took us over the mountains on the highway, grey sky pressing down where sunrise should have been. After a bridge across a valley gorge, a sign for Taipei appeared.

My first impression of Taipei was that it looked old. Not run-down, but worn-in. The kind of grey that takes decades to develop. Covered walkways with small shops underneath, cooking smoke, sesame oil, sewer gas, and frying oil all tangled in the air. The traditional Chinese characters on every sign kept reminding me I was somewhere different, but my nose was telling me I'd been here before.

I asked my wife what she thought. She wasn't impressed. I couldn't stop feeling like I'd been here in a past life.

{{< image src="fuhanq-doujiang.jpg" caption="Fu Hang Dou Jiang — salty soy milk, sesame flatbread, and fried dough sticks" >}}

That feeling kept coming back. When we returned to Taipei after Chiayi and Tainan, walking through the Zhongshan district again, I felt something close to coming home. By the end of the trip I'd gotten used to the constant rain, the rhythm of ducking under covered walkways, even the fact that half the shops don't open until 2 PM. Taipei main station's upper floors at 10 AM: just a lonely FamilyMart. The city runs on its own clock.

## 🏛️ The Palace Museum

The [National Palace Museum](https://maps.google.com/?q=國立故宮博物院+台北) in Taipei was what I was most looking forward to.

We planned half a day. By 1 PM we'd only covered one floor. We rushed upstairs for a mediocre beef noodle soup, then ran back down. We didn't leave until after 4, and we still missed a lot. The famous [Jadeite Cabbage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jadeite_Cabbage) and the Meat-Shaped Stone were both away on loan.

What we did see was enough. Emperor Qianlong's collection is absurd — calligraphy and paintings spanning the Tang through Qing dynasties, ceramics from the Neolithic to the Republic era. Five or six pieces of [secret-color celadon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secret_colour_porcelain). A pair of Chenghua [chicken cups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicken_cup) — the museum has ten of them. Song dynasty [Ru ware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ru_ware), cloisonné, Tang sancai. Apart from Yuan blue-and-white, the entire arc of Chinese ceramics was there. Like raiding Qianlong's personal collection room.

{{< gallery images="cloisonne-vessel.jpg,buddha-statue.jpg" captions="Cloisonné vessel,Buddhist sculpture" >}}

The piece that stopped me was a calligraphy scroll. Emperor Gaozong of Song — Zhao Gou — wrote a personal dispatch to General [Yue Fei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yue_Fei), praising him, encouraging him, calling him indispensable. Zhao Gou was himself a master calligrapher, and you can see the influence of [Wang Xizhi's Orchid Pavilion Preface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lantingji_Xu) in every stroke. Eight months after writing this, he had Yue Fei executed. The names on the scroll — Yue Fei, Wuzhu, Han Shizhong, Zhang Jun — I'd read about all of them since childhood. Standing that close to something nearly a thousand years old, I had trouble breathing. My eyes got hot.

{{< gallery images="yue-fei-scroll.jpg,yue-fei-detail.jpg" captions="Imperial dispatch to Yue Fei,Detail — 'To Yue Fei'" >}}

Days later we visited the [Southern Branch](https://maps.google.com/?q=故宮南院+嘉義) in Chiayi. Newer building, better layout. The Jadeite Cabbage had been moved here permanently to draw visitors.

Two things held me for a long time. One was the original [Treaty of Shimonoseki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Shimonoseki) — the document that ceded Taiwan to Japan in 1895 and reshaped modern Chinese history. What does it mean for Taiwan to be the one holding this? The other was a stone pagoda looted from Shanxi by the Japanese army and taken to the Tokyo museum, returned to the ROC government after the war. But when the Japanese took the pagoda, locals in Shanxi hid the finial. It's still sitting in a museum in Shuozhou. The pagoda and its top have been apart for almost eighty years.

{{< image src="stone-pagoda.jpg" caption="Stone pagoda at the Southern Branch — its finial is still in Shanxi" >}}

## ⛩️ Walking Taipei

{{< image src="longshan-temple.jpg" caption="Longshan Temple" >}}

We caught a prayer ceremony at [Longshan Temple](https://maps.google.com/?q=龍山寺+台北). Chanting in the rain, lanterns still up from the Lantern Festival. We walked from Bangka toward Ximending, passing the restored [Bopiliao](https://maps.google.com/?q=剝皮寮+台北) old street and Qingshui Temple — filming locations from the movie *[Monga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monga_(film))*. A small pilgrimage.

Ximending was smaller than I expected. What surprised me was the density of Japanese tourists — we bought our bubble tea surrounded by them. Browsed a four-story Pop Mart flagship and the [Red House](https://maps.google.com/?q=西門紅樓+台北).

[Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall](https://maps.google.com/?q=中正紀念堂+台北) is worth the visit. The gate reads "Liberty Square" — renamed from "Great Centrality and Perfect Uprightness" in 2007 — but the building inside is still called Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall. Liberty and Chiang facing each other. Make of that what you will. The architecture mimics the Temple of Heaven in Beijing. I read his final testament, still calling for retaking the mainland to the very end. I bowed anyway. The lower level has a small exhibit of his personal effects — documents, letters, uniforms — which my wife and I found absorbing. Taiwan gives equal space to the [White Terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Terror_(Taiwan)) and the [Lei Zhen incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_China_Journal). A terminal plays his recorded speeches — fewer than ten of them. The rest of the memorial has nothing to do with politics: craft markets, art exhibitions, very Japanese aesthetics. The hourly guard-changing ceremony has become performative — there's no actual guard post anymore. The market ring around the memorial was the best part.

[Songshan Cultural Park](https://maps.google.com/?q=松山文創園區+台北) is a converted cigarette factory. We browsed the Eslite mall first. The 24-hour bookstore is real — I picked up Chiang Kai-shek's 1936 diary, because anyone who journals daily gets curious about other people's journals. The warehouse flea market inside the park was packed on a Friday afternoon. Artfully arranged vintage items and cart coffee. Tasteful, but nothing I wanted to buy. My wife felt the same. We couldn't explain it. Good aesthetics, interesting objects, zero purchase desire. This kept happening at Tainan's [Hayashi Department Store](https://maps.google.com/?q=林百貨+台南), the Anping old street, and [Yongkang Street](https://maps.google.com/?q=永康街+台北). Taiwan's creative scene puts in genuine effort. Something about the aesthetic just doesn't land for us.

The one purchase I don't regret: a hand-carved seal. Found the shop on day one, ordered a horn stamp with my name in ancient seal script. Picked it up when we got back to Taipei. NT$3,000. Worth every cent.

{{< image src="seal-stamp.jpg" caption="My horn seal — ancient seal script" >}}

[Taipei 101](https://maps.google.com/?q=台北101) took two attempts. First time the queue was impossible. Second time the weather was bad, so fewer people. Forty-five seconds in the elevator to the observation deck. Squeezed through Korean tour groups for a night view. Every major city's tallest building offers roughly the same experience.

Yongkang Street is pleasant enough. My wife had a shop bookmarked for years. We passed one packed entirely with Japanese customers. Tried to find a vinyl record shop — nothing I wanted. Ended up in a tea house reading. The owner was enthusiastically friendly. My wife thought it was a bit much; I didn't mind.

Last day: [Dihua Street](https://maps.google.com/?q=迪化街+台北), where Taipei locals buy New Year supplies. We wanted souvenirs, found nothing, ate a strawberry daifuku, bought licorice powder and plum powder. Got SunnyHills pineapple cakes from 101 instead. The underground walkway from Zhongshan station to Taipei Main Station is surprisingly useful when it rains.

## 🚂 Going South

High-speed rail to Chiayi, one hour forty minutes. The HSR station is far from the city center — more of a transit hub for tourists.

Chiayi was rougher than I expected. My wife said it was worse than Fengxiang (a small town in our home province). That's an exaggeration, but the sewer smell was stronger and the whole city felt decades behind. No five-star hotels. Our room had traffic noise until midnight. The night market, though — packed solid, as dense as anything in Taipei. Turkey rice was average. Pork liver with ginger was good. The real winner was a guava salad with licorice-lemon seasoning. First bite and I knew: fruit from the south hits different.

The [Taiwan Tile Museum](https://maps.google.com/?q=台灣花磚博物館+嘉義) had an hour-long queue for a two-story house. They're preserving decorative tiles from 1910–1935. Shoes off, limited entry. I couldn't connect with it. My wife loved it. We planned to see the Chiayi Lantern Festival but the shuttle bus queue stretched down the block. Three to four hours round trip. We ate night market food instead.

Chiayi to Tainan on the Tze-Chiang Express, thirty-odd minutes. Arrived and it felt like the city had just woken up. Tainan runs at a different speed from Taipei. Slower, more scattered, like nothing is pressing.

{{< image src="tainan-street.jpg" caption="Tainan streets" >}}

[Hayashi Department Store](https://maps.google.com/?q=林百貨+台南) is Tainan's landmark, a Japanese colonial-era department store. The rooftop still has bullet holes from American bombing runs, and a small Shinto shrine. Everything inside was tasteful and I still couldn't open my wallet.

Tainan's history is more concentrated than Taipei's. I'd assumed [Chikan Tower](https://maps.google.com/?q=赤崁樓+台南)'s name came from its red color — turns out it's a transliteration of an indigenous village name. Inside there are stone tablets from Emperor Qianlong's suppression of the [Lin Shuangwen Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lin_Shuangwen_rebellion). The bigger surprise: Chikan Tower was the old seat of Taiwan Prefecture. So Tainan really is to Taipei what Kyoto is to Tokyo. Tainan is the root.

{{< image src="confucius-temple.jpg" caption="Tainan Confucius Temple" >}}

The [Martial Temple](https://maps.google.com/?q=祀典武廟+台南) shows what Chinese folk religion actually looks like: Buddhist, Taoist, Confucian, and popular deities all sharing the same roof. Guanyin, Guan Yu, the Matchmaker God, the Tiger God. No doctrinal gatekeeping. The [Confucius Temple](https://maps.google.com/?q=臺南孔廟) is in excellent condition. Eight imperial plaques from Kangxi through Guangxu survive here — the most complete set anywhere. Most mainland temples lost theirs in the Cultural Revolution. After the Qing emperors, Chiang Kai-shek and Chiang Ching-kuo added plaques. Then in the democratic era, Chen Shui-bian, Ma Ying-jeou, Tsai Ing-wen, and Lai Ching-te each followed. Every leader hangs a plaque to claim legitimacy. That tradition, at least, has been unbroken.

{{< image src="tianhou-temple.jpg" caption="Luermen Tianhou Temple" >}}

[Anping Fort](https://maps.google.com/?q=安平古堡+台南) is surprisingly small for something 450 years old. But I learned something: the name "Taiwan" comes from "Tayouan" — the sandbar where Anping Fort sits, which was the big bay that eventually silted up and connected to the mainland. The old street nearby had school field-trip energy — kids everywhere. We ate shrimp rolls (good, not remarkable) and a tofu pudding topped with mixed beans (beautiful).

{{< image src="lanterns.jpg" caption="Tainan lanterns" >}}

[Sicao Green Tunnel](https://maps.google.com/?q=四草綠色隧道+台南) was the first natural scenery of the trip. Mangrove branches on both sides of the waterway grow toward the light reflecting off the surface, crossing overhead to form a tunnel. We floated through on a flat-bottomed boat. Genuinely pretty. The nearby museum has a mother-and-calf sperm whale specimen. The mother's fin was broken by a boat propeller; she beached and died. The calf refused to eat and followed.

Two days in Tainan and we agreed: lovely to walk around, but not as much to do as we'd hoped. Maybe we didn't plan enough. Or maybe Tainan's appeal is precisely the aimless wandering and eating, no agenda required.

## 🔙 Back in Taipei

Our last meal in Tainan was dumplings my wife picked — a place called Happy Potstickers. Zero expectations. Turns out a plain vegetable potsticker can be extraordinary when the skin is that crispy. Tainan pricing is something else: a cup of coffee costs as much as three dishes at breakfast.

HSR back to Taipei. Rain, naturally. The remaining days were packed with things we'd missed.

Danshui first. My wife wanted to see Jay Chou's alma mater and the filming location from *[Secret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secret_(2007_film))*. Danshui sits on the foothills of Yangmingshan — the school is up a hill. Classes were in session, no visitors. We took photos at the gate and found the red wall from the movie in a side alley. Went down to the old street for breakfast. Most shops closed. We stumbled into a wet-market alley instead — deep-frying, seafood, blood pudding smells mixing together. Bought nothing. The atmosphere reminded me of the market streets back home.

{{< image src="misty-beitou.jpg" caption="Secret filming spot" >}}

Beitou for hot springs at [Marshal Zen Garden](https://maps.google.com/?q=少帥禪園+北投). The place has layers: first a rest station for kamikaze pilots during the Japanese occupation, then a residence where [Zhang Xueliang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Xueliang) was held for over a decade. The sulfur smell is strong, the water milky white. My first time in this kind of spring. Private bath, a cold plunge pool next to it. The sensation of jumping between scalding and freezing is extraordinary — re-entering the hot water feels like a thousand tiny needles, then pure relief. Afterward we walked the garden. The view over Beitou toward Danshui is beautiful. Zhang Xueliang probably didn't enjoy it.

Beef noodle soup had been on my mind since we arrived. Nearly a week in and still hadn't had any. Went to [Tian Xia San Jue](https://maps.google.com/?q=天下三絕牛肉麵+台北), a place that shows up on every list. It was fine. Nothing that made me stop chewing. Maybe my palate had gotten too demanding.

The last night we found a gua bao shop that actually serves Taiwanese oden and small plates. Ordered too much. The chicken cartilage was the surprise — so crispy it dissolved on contact. The owner was warm without being overbearing. My wife said that's the kind of hospitality she likes. Finished after nine.

## 🏮 Jiufen and Shifen

Jiufen and Shifen come bundled with any Taipei trip. We did a chartered day tour: Jiufen, then Shifen, ending at [Raohe Night Market](https://maps.google.com/?q=饒河夜市+台北).

At the foot of the Jiufen mountains, where the river meets the sea, is the [Yin-Yang Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yin-Yang_Sea) — iron from the old Jinguashi gold mines oxidizes in the seawater, leaving half the bay yellow-brown and half blue-green. Worth seeing from a distance. Jiufen itself is commercialized to the point of exhaustion. The shop ladies switch between Mandarin, Japanese, Korean, and English without blinking. Impressive linguistically, less charming commercially. We ate popiah ice cream rolls with peanut candy and cilantro, [Lai A Po taro balls](https://maps.google.com/?q=賴阿婆芋圓+九份) (chewy, but at the end of the day it's sugar water), and had tea at the iconic [A-Mei Tea House](https://maps.google.com/?q=阿妹茶樓+九份). Jiufen started as a mining town during the Japanese occupation. Its original prosperity was the red-light district kind.

{{< image src="jiufen.jpg" caption="Jiufen — A-Mei Tea House" >}}

Shifen is a railway running through an old street. People come to release sky lanterns. You can write on four sides. I wrote two lines:

> 人间有味是清欢 — Su Shi (The truest flavor in life is plain joy)

> 好风凭借力，送我上青云 — Xue Baochai, *Dream of the Red Chamber* (A good wind, when it comes, will carry me to the clouds)

A small blessing for us.

## 🍜 Eating Our Way Through

We knew there'd be a lot of eating. We didn't expect that nearly every day would include something we'd never tasted before.

Vermicelli soup was the biggest surprise. I'd never understood the appeal. Then I tried [Mianxian Chuanqi](https://maps.google.com/?q=面線傳奇+台北). The braised intestine was rich and tender with no off-flavors. The oysters were sweet without the funk. The gluey vermicelli base, plus garlic, chili sauce, black vinegar, white pepper — it all works. We went back on our last day. Still good.

{{< image src="mianxian.jpg" caption="Mianxian Chuanqi" >}}

Night markets are where Taiwan eats. [Ningxia Night Market](https://maps.google.com/?q=寧夏夜市+台北) had fried potato balls that were unreasonably good. [Nanmen Night Market](https://maps.google.com/?q=南機場夜市+台北)'s Shanei chicken rice taught me that cold chicken can be exceptional — springy, fragrant, with angelica root soup and sticky rice on the side. The nameless pushcart selling sesame flatbreads was something else: bite-sized, salty-crispy and red-bean, four of them in my stomach before I noticed.

{{< gallery images="fried-potato-balls.jpg,chicken-rice.jpg" captions="Fried potato balls at Ningxia,Shanei chicken rice at Nanmen" >}}

Raohe Night Market wasn't on the plan. It ended up producing the best bites of the trip. The Michelin pepper bun at the entrance — one bite and I was gone. You can't take these home. I'll miss them. Maqao sausage was new: maqao is the indigenous Taiwanese name for mountain pepper (*Litsea cubeba*), and it gives the sausage a lemony fragrance I'd never encountered. Ice-fire tangyuan puts steaming-hot rice balls on shaved ice, creating a dry-ice effect when it arrives. Finish with osmanthus honey and lemon juice. On the way out, a NT$50 scallion pancake with basil and egg — even stuffed, I could eat one more.

{{< image src="raohe-entrance.jpg" caption="Raohe Street Night Market" >}}

Tainan's food was even more memorable. Milkfish ball soup was the first thing we ate there, and it immediately reset my impression of the city. The fish skin and belly were flavors I'd never experienced. Guoshao yimian — a noodle dish unique to Tainan where the noodles are deep-fried first — had us queueing for ages. A small bowl, not enough. [Lily Fruit Shop](https://maps.google.com/?q=莉莉水果店+台南)'s fruit shaved ice on a warm afternoon with a breeze — that's the kind of moment that stays.

{{< image src="milkfish-spread.jpg" caption="Milkfish spread — braised pork rice, intestine, and greens" >}}

{{< gallery images="guoshao-noodles.jpg,douhua-ice.jpg,fish-soup.jpg" captions="Tainan guoshao noodles,Lily Fruit Shop shaved ice,Yimalinei fresh fish soup" >}}

What I miss most: the intestine at Mianxian Chuanqi, the pepper buns at Raohe, and Tainan's milkfish. And the salty soy milk at [Fu Hang Dou Jiang](https://maps.google.com/?q=阜杭豆漿+台北) — a texture somewhere between egg custard and tofu pudding — probably can't be found outside Taipei.

## 🛬 After

Taiwan felt different from every other country I've visited. There was no thrill of encountering an alien culture. Instead, a strange déjà vu. The shape of the streets, the smell of the temples, the night market chaos, the layered scent of the air. All familiar, yet constantly reminding me this isn't the mainland.

What stayed with me most wasn't the scenery. It was the layered history. Thousand-year-old calligraphy in the Palace Museum. Imperial plaques in the Confucius Temple spanning from Kangxi to the current president. The contradictions inside Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall. Chikan Tower built on Dutch, then Qing, then Japanese, then ROC foundations. Every corner tells you this land has been through too much. And the Taiwanese keep living on top of all of it — praying, night-marketing, queueing, riding scooters, opening their shops at 2 PM.

Ten days. About 180,000 steps. Two Palace Museums, one hot spring, one sky lantern. Chiayi was rough but the night market was loud. Tainan was so relaxed it made me sleepy. Taipei never stopped raining.

We landed in Melbourne at noon, showered, and passed out. Woke up still exhausted. That always happens after a trip. But sitting at home, all I could think about was the intestine vermicelli, the pepper buns, and that calligraphy scroll where an emperor praised a general he was about to kill.

Next time, I'm seeing the calligraphy and Song dynasty landscapes I missed.
